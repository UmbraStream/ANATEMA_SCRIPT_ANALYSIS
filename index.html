00Overview

The Script At A Glance

ANATEMA is the middle panel of a mythic triptych, and it reads like one — a descent narrative structured around a mother who has been diving toward a lost daughter for eight years, a cosmology in which the Greek experiment of civilization is ending, and a sea that does not take but keeps. The screenplay is ambitious in scope, restrained in execution, and unusually honest about grief. It works more often than it doesn’t, and where it works it works powerfully.

This document is organized by act. Within each act, strengths are catalogued first — because there are many, and because the screenplay’s strengths are distinctive enough to warrant specific attention — followed by weak points and concrete suggestions for revision. A final verdict summarizes the piece’s standing as both a standalone film and as the middle movement of THE GREEK COLLISION.

The reason this screenplay works, when it works, is that it has understood something most screenplays about grief do not: the sea does not give things back, and that has to be okay, and the way it becomes okay is not through closure but through continuation.

Central Thesis of the Script

Loss is not resolved — it is inherited. Luna’s signal runs in the water forever. Mara’s signal joins it. The frequency is permanent. The sea keeps what it keeps, and that keeping is not consolation but cosmology. This is a thesis most screenplays about grief cannot reach; this one does.

Principal Dramatic Assets

A protagonist whose want and need are identical (to reach her daughter / to accept that her daughter is not returnable); an antagonist whose evil is bureaucratic rather than malicious; a supporting cast structured around a descent-ladder of human limits; and a final image that refuses reunion and earns its refusal.

01The Zone Opens

Act One  ·  Pages 1 – 33  ·  Setup, Inciting Incident, Commitment

Act One is the screenplay’s most economical work. In roughly thirty-three pages it establishes a protagonist, a wound, a world, a cosmology, an antagonist, a ticking clock, and a team — without any of these feeling pre-fabricated. The opening sequence in particular is doing work most screenplays attempt and fail.

STRENGTHThe Opening Sequence Is Load-Bearing And Earns Everything

Mara and six-year-old Luna on the dawn water, the daughter’s quiet “Me too,” the forty-seconds-too-late rescue dive, and the cut to eight years later — these four pages earn the entire emotional stakes of the film. The specific image of Luna not falling but descending, the way Mara descends, with direction, transforms a drowning into an inheritance. This is the single most important craft decision in the screenplay: the loss is not a random tragedy but a hereditary call, which makes everything that follows organic.

What It Buys You
Permanent emotional purchase on Mara

By the end of page 6, the audience is aligned with Mara in the way that usually takes a full first act to achieve. The script has bought itself the right to be quiet, slow, and strange in later passages because the engine is already running.

STRENGTHApollo As Diagnostic Antagonist

Apollo’s entrance on the south shore rocks is one of the strongest scenes in the screenplay. The choice to make him beautiful and wrong in the way of something that has been perfect so long it forgot what imperfection was for is genuinely original. He is not menacing; he is diagnostic. His sensor array reads heart rate and cortisol. His voice arrives before his mouth moves. His answer to why he let Luna drown — “The data was necessary” — is more chilling than any villain monologue could be. This is evil rendered as bureaucracy, which is harder to write and more honest than evil rendered as cruelty.

STRENGTHThe Meteorite / Zone Inciting Incident

The landing — “Not explosion. Reception. The water opening, taking it, closing.” — is three lines of description that tell the audience the entire genre in six words. The subsequent collapse of every human-made frequency in a forty-kilometer radius converts a science-fiction premise into a mythic one: civilization goes briefly dark, and under that darkness the older signal becomes audible. The Fisherman sitting down because he knows something has come home is a perfect gestural beat.

STRENGTHVesna, Introduced Without Fanfare

“I’m not diving. I’m holding the boats. Someone has to stay on the surface and watch.” This is a character stating her structural function in the story without the screenplay telegraphing it. Vesna is the moral spine of ANATEMA — the one who stays on the surface while the protagonist goes through — and Act One introduces her with exactly the economy the part requires.

STRENGTHThe Seven Sails, The Ancient Technology

The image of seven wind-powered catamarans assembling at dawn because anything with a motor fails inside the zone is doing quiet thematic work: the film’s solution to a post-human crisis is pre-industrial. The world is saved by rope, sail, and breath. This is a choice, not a coincidence, and it pays off formally in the final image of Vesna rowing back.

WEAK POINTThe Countdown Does Not Yet Compete With The Dive

The fourteen-hour / eleven-hour / nine-hour zone-expansion clock is technically functional but dramatically diffuse. The script’s real clock is oxygen in Mara’s lungs, and the external countdown competes with the internal one rather than amplifying it. A 40-km zone reaching the coast is abstract; a woman at 290m with five seconds of breath is not.

Suggested Revision
Let the external clock recede once the dive begins

Establish the zone’s expansion firmly in Act One — it is doing useful work there, giving Romel’s NATO presence a reason to assemble the sailboats — then deliberately lower its volume once the divers cross the perimeter. The film’s second-act tension is not will they beat the zone but will Mara reach the mirror. Cutting back to Romel checking his watch in Act Two dilutes rather than intensifies. Consider cutting Romel’s mid-act interludes entirely and returning to him only at the zone’s collapse.

WEAK POINTRomel Is Still A Type, Not A Person

“Get me every free diver in NATO’s personnel registry. And get me sailboats.” is an excellent line, but Romel himself is The Competent Commander with no interior specificity. The script earns him time because the countdown is real; he does not yet use that time.

Suggested Revision
Give Romel one specific, human weight

One line of private history — a previous operation on this coast, a son, a fear of deep water — is all that is needed. Not backstory; a single anchor. The screenplay trusts its actors elsewhere; it can trust them here too.

WEAK POINTThe Mythology Arrives Asserted Rather Than Earned

“Before Zeus. Before the blueprint.” — “Three thousand years of the blueprint — undone.” A reader encountering ANATEMA without MENIS will do significant inferential work to assemble the cosmology. What is the experiment? What is Apollo maintaining? Why was Poseidon exiled? The words are evocative; the referents are underspecified.

Suggested Revision
One concrete image of the blueprint

Apollo does not need to explain — that would ruin him — but a single visual beat could anchor the cosmology. Consider: during the south-shore confrontation, Apollo’s sensor array briefly projects (visibly, onto the rock, onto the water, into Mara’s line of sight) the lattice he maintains. One image. No dialogue. The audience now has a referent for “the blueprint” for the rest of the film.

02The Descent

Act Two  ·  Pages 34 – 56  ·  Crossing, First Contact, The Mirror

Act Two is where ANATEMA stops being ambitious and becomes achieved. The descent sequence — each diver stopping at a different depth, for a different reason, and each stop externalizing a different human relationship to limit — is the finest writing in the screenplay and the most original structural choice. If the film has a signature, it is here.

STRENGTHThe Ladder Of Depths

Cole at 120 meters (honest, no protocol, ascends without shame). Petra at 150 meters (feels present for the first time in twelve competitive years, cries on a breath she cannot waste). Ana at 200 meters (came for transcendence, finds only truth, which is larger). Sonja at 240 meters (sets down thirty years of grief in the right place). Lena at 270 meters (releases her final breath-hold reserve as a gift, collapsing three frequencies into one). This is the screenplay teaching itself. Each diver is a different answer to the same question — how far are you willing to go, and why — and each stop is dramatically distinct.

Why It Works
The descent doubles as characterization

Four supporting characters introduced briefly in Act One each receive their most revealing moment in the silence of a single underwater beat. No dialogue is required. The screenplay has earned the right to wordless characterization because Act One laid the groundwork so efficiently.

STRENGTHVesna Holds The Rope

The surface sequence — Apollo’s creatures approaching the boats, the nearest one opening its mouth and playing Luna’s exact voice at age two, the word she invented — is a hair-raising scene, and Vesna’s response in old Čakavian, “You are not her”, is one of the great lines of the screenplay. Her refusal is the ethical mirror of Mara’s descent: Mara reaches the real signal, Vesna refuses the false one. This structural parallel is earned and devastating.

STRENGTHLena’s Exhale At 270 Meters

The choice to have Lena release the last of her breath-hold reserve as a deliberate gift — Not accident — choice — and to have that frequency join Mara’s descent path is the screenplay’s most elegant use of its own premise. The pull is a bloodline. Bloodlines can be given. This beat could not exist in a lesser version of this script.

STRENGTHThe Mirror

Three hundred and thirty-three meters. The membrane. Two worlds visible simultaneously. Poseidon’s hand around Mara’s. Five seconds of oxygen. The image is mythic, but the execution is physical — a hand, a breath, a threshold. This is the moment the screenplay has been building toward since page one, and it does not flinch.

WEAK POINT“Luna Was Never There” Is Told, Not Dramatized

At 290 meters, with five seconds of oxygen, Apollo transmits directly into Mara’s nervous system: the signal she has followed for eight years is grief shaping passage frequency, not her daughter. Mara reasons her way through it — the pull is real, it was real before Luna, real after, hers, not designed — and pushes through. Philosophically correct. Thematically essential. Dramatically, however, it is a paragraph of interior monologue at the climactic beat, which is the one place the screenplay cannot afford interior monologue.

Suggested Revision
Externalize the decision into a single physical action

Consider: Mara reaches into her pocket for the photograph of Luna she has carried the entire film. She looks at it underwater. She lets it go — not as dismissal, as release. The photograph drifts down ahead of her through the mirror. She follows it. The decision is now an action, not a thought. The audience sees her choose the reality of the signal over the image of her daughter, and the photograph becomes the first thing to pass through the membrane. No monologue required.

WEAK POINTApollo’s Transmission Risks Ambiguity

When Apollo sends the “Luna was never there” transmission, is he lying, telling the truth, or telling a truth weaponized for effect? The script leaves this productively ambiguous — Mara concludes it does not matter — but the ambiguity creates a secondary problem: it lowers Apollo’s stakes. If he might be lying, he is less terrifying. If he is telling the truth, the dive has been a grief-projection for eight years. Neither reading is cleanly load-bearing.

Suggested Revision
Commit to one reading and let the other reverberate

The stronger choice is almost certainly: Apollo is telling a partial truth as a weapon. Luna’s signal did dissolve into the passage frequency — that is what Mara finds on the other side. But Apollo’s framing (“she was never there”) is a cruelty, not a fact. This can be clarified with a single line later — Poseidon on the other shore: “What she carried is here. He knew this. He showed you the fact as an absence.”

WEAK POINTCole’s Ascent Is Slightly Too Clean

Cole turning back at 120 meters because he has always had a protocol and here there is no protocol is a beautiful beat, but the screenplay gives him the narrator’s verdict on his own choice: He does not feel bad about this. He feels honest. This is the script telling the audience how to read him. He is interesting enough that he does not need the endorsement.

Suggested Revision
Trust the image; cut the verdict

Let him ascend. Let the next shot be him on the catamaran, quiet, watching the water. The audience will read “honest” without being told. The same note applies to several of the other descent-stops, though less urgently.

03What Is Kept

Act Three  ·  Pages 57 – 82  ·  The Other Side, The Seven Nodes, The Rowing Back

Act Three has two climaxes — the plot climax (Apollo’s last move, the seventh node, the passage fully opening) and the emotional climax (the six catamarans rowing back, one fewer than went out). The screenplay is right to make the second one the true ending. Vesna is where the film’s heart is, and the final image of her feet in the water, watching the patterns, saying “I know”, is exactly the ending the screenplay has been building toward. The concern is that the plot climax currently risks feeling like an obligation to resolve.

STRENGTHThe Refusal To Resurrect Luna

This is the single most important decision in the entire screenplay and it is correct. Mara does not find her daughter. She finds what her daughter carried — the signal, running in the water of Poseidon’s ocean, permanent because water is permanent. “She is not okay in the way of someone returned to before loss. That is not what the sea keeps. She is okay in the way of someone who found what they were looking for and it was not what they expected and it is real and it will have to be enough. Which it is.” That passage is the entire thesis of the film, it is earned, and a lesser version of this screenplay would have reunited them.

Why It Matters
The film’s cosmology becomes coherent here

The sea keeps, it does not give back. The signal is inherited, not recovered. Luna is not a ghost and not a destination; she is a frequency in the medium that connects all seas. This is the cosmology the script has been working toward since page one, and it lands.

STRENGTHApollo’s Admission

“I knew what she was from the moment she went in. The signal was perfect. Complete. The cleanest reading in thirty years of watching your bloodline. I should have let her surface.” Whether this is genuine registration or another calculated transmission (see Act Two notes), the line itself is strong. It is the first time Apollo has spoken in anything other than measurement, and it costs him structurally — his broadcast collapses moments later.

STRENGTHThe Six Catamarans Rowing North

Six catamarans rowing north. Not seven. The absence present the way a frequency is present after the source stops. This is the best single line in Act Three. The screenplay does not mourn Mara — it makes her absence a presence, which is the same operation the screenplay has performed on Luna for the entire film. Structurally elegant.

STRENGTHVesna’s Final “I Know”

The final scene — Vesna on the dock the next dawn, feet in the water, watching the patterns, smiling, saying “I know” to the water — is a perfect closing image. She is the film’s witness. She held the surface. She earned this. The screenplay ends on her and is correct to end on her.

WEAK POINTThe Seven Nodes Sequence Is Structurally Compressed

Mara touches six of the seven nodes between Scene One and Scene Two of Act Three, essentially offscreen. Then the seventh is the confrontation. This is the screenplay skipping the work: it tells us she did the difficult thing, and then dramatizes the difficult thing that mattered. Either the first six deserve dramatization — even briefly, the way the descent dramatizes each depth — or the architecture should be simplified so that the seventh-node confrontation is the whole task.

Suggested Revision — Option A
Collapse the seven into one

Simplify the architecture. The broadcast has a single structural seal, not seven distributed ones. Mara reaches it, touches it, and Apollo’s transmission arrives at that single moment. The dramatic weight concentrates. The seven is biblical in flavor but unnecessary; one node is cinematic.

Suggested Revision — Option B
Dramatize six of seven as a descent-ladder echo

If the seven is structurally required (perhaps for ANABASIS), treat the six as a brief sequence — each node a different texture, the way each depth-stop in Act Two was a different texture. A half-page of dramatized nodes would make the seventh’s reinforcement feel like an escalation rather than an asserted obstacle.

WEAK POINTThree Closing Epigraphs Is One Too Many (Or Two)

“What we do in life echoes in eternity” (Maximus, Gladiator) — “What the sea keeps echoes longer” (the film’s own thesis) — “Only reality is holy, my friend” (Ginsberg / returning epigraph). Each of these is good on its own. In sequence they diminish each other. The middle epigraph is the one the film has earned; the other two are unearned borrowing of pre-existing gravity.

Suggested Revision
Close on the film’s own sentence

Keep “What the sea keeps echoes longer.” Cut the other two. The screenplay has written its own epitaph — it does not need to quote Ridley Scott or Allen Ginsberg to end.

WEAK POINTJuda Is Under-Integrated Across The Whole Script

Juda is doing important work — he is the film’s Cassandra, the witness-on-land who measures what nobody else can measure and who writes the post-experimental language in the final image. Across three acts, however, he does mathematics at a telescope, makes one radio call to Romel, and falls asleep at his desk. As symbol he is effective. As character he is thin.

Suggested Revision
Give Juda one plot-critical moment in Act Two

His measurements should matter to someone’s decision. Consider: at the Act Two midpoint, his radio call to Babić (not Romel) changes the mission — the divers learn something from his mathematics that they could not have known from underwater. Perhaps his math reveals that the passage will close permanently if Mara does not reach the mirror within a specific window, collapsing the external countdown into the internal one and solving the first weak point noted in Act One. Juda becomes the bridge between the surface and the depth — which is thematically his role anyway.

WEAK POINTThe Morgan / Babić Phone Call Spells The Theme

“Tell him it was never a threat. It was a door. Someone on this island was born to open it.” This is a beautiful line and a theme-in-dialogue. The audience has just watched this. The screenplay is telling them what they have seen.

Suggested Revision
Trust the image; shorten the line

Keep “It was a door.” Cut the two sentences around it. The three words are enough. The scene is stronger for the silence.

04Cross-Cutting Observations

Patterns That Span All Three Acts
Voice
The prose of the scene description is distinctive

Fragmented, declarative, often single lines that function as prose poetry. “The sea before the tourists wake.”  —  “Not explosion. Reception.”  —  “Dark. Cold. Crushing. Completely ordinary. Completely changed.” Writing that trusts the reader and trusts the image.

Ethics
Grief is treated with unusual seriousness

The screenplay does not pathologize Mara’s eight-year dive, and it does not romanticize it either. It treats her grief as a cosmological fact, not a problem to solve. This is the ethical posture of the film and it is correct.

Pattern
Thematic statements occasionally close scenes the image has already closed

Cole’s honesty, Vesna’s closing phone line, the three epigraphs, several descent-stop verdicts. The script’s best instinct is image; its recurring weakness is trusting the image slightly less than it should.

Pattern
Supporting characters are thin outside their descent beats

Petra, Ana, Cole, Sonja are archetypes (Champion, Mystic, Soldier, Archaeologist) rendered beautifully in their single depth-stop moment but almost invisible otherwise. One additional line of specificity each, placed in Act One, would compound across the descent.

A Note On The Trilogy Frame

Several of this document’s weak-point notes — the under-specified cosmology, Juda’s thin integration, the seven-node architecture — are almost certainly load-bearing in MENIS or ANABASIS. The analysis above treats ANATEMA as a standalone film because that is the discipline a middle panel must survive: if it only works as part of the triptych, it does not work as itself. The strengths catalogued here, by contrast, are entirely the screenplay’s own.

A Note On Dialogue

The dialogue is, on the whole, excellent. The best lines are restrained to the point of austerity: “I know.”  —  “Go.”  —  “Sails up.”  —  “You are not her.” The screenplay achieves its most powerful effects with two to three words. When it slips, it slips because it has allowed itself more.

Final Verdict

A mythic screenplay that earns its cosmology.

ANATEMA is an unusual piece of work: a grief film disguised as a science-fiction film, or a science-fiction film disguised as a mythology, or — more accurately than either — a screenplay that refuses the distinction. It has an uncommonly strong protagonist, an original antagonist, a supporting ensemble used with real structural intelligence, and an ending that refuses the cheap resolution and earns the harder one.

Its weaknesses are real and addressable. The mythology needs one more concrete anchor. The seven-node architecture needs either dramatization or simplification. Juda needs a plot-critical beat. The closing epigraphs need pruning. Several dialogue passages need to trust their images more. None of these are structural defects. All of them are the kind of weakness that is fixed in a revision rather than a rewrite.

The script is good. In its descent sequence and its final image it is better than good. With the revisions suggested here, it would be the strongest middle panel a triptych could ask for.
